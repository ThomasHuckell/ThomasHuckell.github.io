Masters Degree

Legged robots have the potential to traverse remote or dangerous areas by utilizing intermittent ground contact to navigate through complex terrain. Despite their promise, legged robots, specifically bipeds, are inherently unstable and fail to operate reliably in unstructured environments. This thesis aims to improve the standing balancing of bipedal robots through the development of a new unified model that incorporates three main fixed base balancing strategies of ankle, hip, and variable height.

Thesis Overview

Legged robots are complex systems with a high degree of freedom which make optimal control methodologies for the whole-body dynamics computationally complex, especially for planning over long time horizons. Reduced order models encapsulate the whole-body dynamics into lower degree of freedom representation, making it more suitable for long term planning. The solutions of the reduced order models can then be fed back to whole-body control for objectives for to execute.

The existing reduced order models used in legged robots only consider two of the three available balancing strategies:

    ankle strategy: modulate center of pressure within the base of support.
    hip strategy: generate angular momentum about center of mass to produce restoring shear force at base of support.
    variable height strategy: use vertical acceleration of center of mass to momentarily increase ground reaction force magnitude.

In my research I propose variable height inverted pendulum plus flywheel (VHIPPFW) model a reduced order model that includes all available strategies with each of its control inputs relating to ankle, hip and variable height strategies.

With this unified model we examined how access to all balancing strategies impacts push recovery performance. The key contributions of my research was examining the stability regions of existing models to examine the contribution each balancing strategy has on overall stability, push recovery simulation using trajectory optimization of the unified model against existing models, and finally developed a nonlinear model predictive controller based on the unified and existing reduced order models then examining each controllers performance on push recovery simulations of the reduced order model dynamics as well as being implemented on simple simulated one legged robot.

Stability Analysis

For the stability analysis we examined the linear inverted pendulum (LIP), linear inverted pendulum plus flywheel (LIPFW), and variable height inverted pendulum (VHIP) models. These models correspond to an ankle, ankle + hip, and ankle + variable height balancing strategy. We use anthropomorphic model parameters to gain insight on human balancing and better elucidate each strategies utility in standing balance for legged robots.

Previous research has been done to analytically solve the stability region for the LIP and LIPPFW model. VHIP model stability region cumbersome to solve analytically due to the nonlinearity. We determine the VHIP stability region numerically through examining the maximum control effort a bang-bang control where the model accelerates as fast as it can then quickly decelerates so the model reaches maximum height and momentarily increases the ground reaction force. After model reaches its maximum height, stability is achieved only if the if LIP stability criterion is satisfied. To compare the VHIP stability region we simulate the VHIP using the bang-bang control scheme over dense grid of initial conditions, then use a binary search of the final states of VHIP to determine if the LIP stability criterion is satisfied. The boundary set of stable initial conditions was taken as the stability region.

The ankle is strategy is having largest impact on the stability region, followed by the hip and variable height strategies. The hip and variable height strategies are constrained by joint limits of physical systems limiting the amount of time these strategies can be applied limiting their efficacy. While hip strategy is independent of initial condition, the variable height strategy is dependent on initial condition. The variable height strategy principle is based on momentarily increasing the ground reaction force, the component of this force which that is restoring balance is dependant difference between the center of mass and the center of pressure. As center of pressure approaches the center of mass the component of the restoring force reduces reducing the impact of the vertical acceleration.

Trajectory optimization

We examined push recovery of the existing and proposed unified model by simulating the model dynamics when exposed to disturbance force using trajectory optimization. We transcribed the continous time problem into a nonlinear program using trapizoidal direct colocation method and used an optimization criterion that equally penalizes the state deviation from the rest state and cost of control. With this criterion we are were able to encourage the optimization to select balancing strategy that is most efficient at recovering from distrubances.
